Brief History of Hilongos

12th Century

According to local folklore, a powerful chieftan from Iloilo named Amahawin conquered the western shores of Leyte and formed a settlement. He named the settlement Hilongos because its inhabitants were Ilonggos.

1590

The settlement was formally founded as a town under Spanish colonial rule and became known as Pueblo de los Hilongos.

1710

The Jesuits established a residence there. It is not exactly known when Hilongos became a parish, some sources say it happened approximately from before 1737 to 1754.

1768

The Jesuits ceded Hilongos to the Augustinians. In 1774-79, the Augustinians established schools in Hilongos.

1784

Palompon, a Hilongos visita, became an independent parish.

1862

Manicar led a revolt against the Spanish at barrio Santa Margarita.

1873

Leovio Magia led a revolt. Unlike the towns of eastern Leyte, which were ceded to the Franciscans in 1843, the towns along Leyte's western coast gradually fell to the seculars.

1900

American forces arrived in Hilongos and were met with strong local resistance. Captain Franscisco Flordelis (Capitan Kikoy) engaged the Americans in a bloody encounter before retreating to establish guerrilla military headquarters in Barrio Pulta (now Sta. Margarita).

Defense and Resistance

Throughout its history, the town maintained a strong defensive and independent posture. During the Spanish era, the historic church complex was built with bastioned fortifications to protect the town from coastal raids. This defiant spirit carried over into the Philippine-American War in 1900, when local leaders like Capitan Francisco Flordelis engaged American forces in bloody encounters and established guerrilla headquarters in the local barrios.

Church Complex

The town's historic church was built with a bell tower attributed to Don Leonardo Celis-Díaz, a native of Cebu. The original church structure also functioned as a bastioned fortification to defend against raids, and remnants of these defensive walls and bastions still exist today.
